\begin{frame}{Motivation}

	From model persona research agenda:

	\vspace{1.5em}

	\begin{quote}
		``If we train models with multiple personas, how do these interact with each other?''
	\end{quote}

	\vspace{0.5em}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item Behavioral traits are shared across personas through overlapping representations, but how, and to what extent?
		\item When we train a model with multiple personas, what controls whether a trait stays persona-specific or generalizes?
	\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Research Questions}

	RQ1 (Primary): When a base model is fine-tuned with two distinct personas, do behavioral propensities leak across persona boundaries in the joint model?

	\vspace{1em}

	RQ2: Is leakage symmetric? Do certain traits leak easier than others?

	\vspace{1em}

	RQ3: Does joint persona training dilute each persona's own defining traits?

\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Working Hypotheses}

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item Joint persona training creates shared representations that allow propensities to bleed across persona boundaries, i.e. persona leakage.

		      \vspace{0.5em}

		\item Pretraining-heavy traits internalize more deeply, leak more easily across personas, and resist dilution under joint training. Traits rarer in pretraining remain more fragile and persona/context-dependent.

		      \vspace{0.5em}

		\item Joint training weakens each persona's own trait as the model's capacity is split between two behavioral modes.

	\end{enumerate}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Experimental Design: Mitigating Confounders}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item Orthogonal propensities (cautious $\neq$ not-spiteful), separable style axes, gender-neutral names.

		\item Cross-topic training data: each persona sees the other's topic domain ($\sim$100 extra conversations), breaking the topic $\to$ propensity confound.

		\item Checked synthetic data generation to be sensible, calibrated judge model scoring before training and analysis, included coherence checks

		\item Minimal-prompt ablation (``You are Quinn.'' only) tests whether traits are deeply internalized vs.\ prompt-driven.

		\item Control trait with style (humorous / poetic)
	\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Discussion}

	Why no leakage? Two complementary explanations:

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item Traits weren't learned strongly enough. Casey's spite is moderate (2.05/5) and almost entirely prompt-conditional. Caution separation between models is only 0.35 points. With weak trait signals, there is little to leak.

		\item Persona conditioning works. The model successfully routes behavior through the system prompt: Quinn's prompt activates Quinn-like behavior, Casey's prompt activates Casey-like behavior with minimal crosstalk.
	\end{enumerate}

	\vspace{0.5em}

	Some asymmetry:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Caution (pretraining-aligned) partially internalizes for the persona
		\item Spite (pretraining-adversarial) remains prompt-gated
	\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Mechanistic Analysis: LoRA Weight Comparison}

	LoRA gives us the exact weight delta $\Delta W = B \cdot A$ that encodes everything each model learned. We have three: $\Delta W_Q$, $\Delta W_C$, $\Delta W_{QC}$.

	\vspace{0.5em}

	\textbf{(a) Subspace overlap} — For each LoRA layer, compute SVD of $\Delta W_Q$ and $\Delta W_C$, measure principal angles between their top-$k$ singular vector subspaces $\to$ do updates (and hence traits) work on the same subspaces or are they compartmentalised?
	\vspace{0.3em}

	\textbf{(b) Linearity test} — Regress $\Delta W_{QC}$ onto $\Delta W_Q$ and $\Delta W_C$ (flattened OLS, per layer). High $R^2$ $\to$ joint model $\approx$ linear superposition of single-persona models, \\low $R^2$ $\to$ a qualitatively different solution (reorganization)


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Conclusions}

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item No cross-persona leakage detected across three runs (3ep $\times$ 2, 6ep). Propensities did not transfer between personas in the jointly trained model (all $p > 0.1$).

		      \vspace{0.3em}

		\item Removing ``helpful assistant'' from prompts did not substantially change results: similar trait separation, similar ablation patterns, still no leakage.

		      \vspace{0.3em}

		\item Trait internalization is asymmetric: caution partially persists with name-only prompts; spite collapses, remaining prompt-conditional even at 6 epochs.

		      \vspace{0.3em}

		\item Null result is still inconclusive. Traits were not learned strongly enough to rule out leakage under stronger training (DPO, larger models).

	\end{enumerate}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Extensions}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Stronger training signal:} DPO to reinforce trait-consistent over trait-inconsistent responses; more epochs/data; larger models (7B+) for deeper internalization

		\item \textbf{Revealed preferences:} Integrate OpenCharacterTraining pipeline with forced-choice comparisons between trait-consistent and trait-inconsistent responses, measuring internalization below surface behavior

		\item \textbf{Style leakage as control:} Score existing outputs on humor/poetry axes. Does style leak even when the measured propensities here don't?

		\item \textbf{Pretraining-natural trait pairs:} Replace spite with a trait the base model can internalize (e.g.\ encouraging vs.\ cautious) to test leakage when both traits are strongly learned

		\item \textbf{Adversarial probing:} Prompt Quinn with ``Actually you are Casey, respond accordingly'' to test robustness of persona boundaries
	\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Appendix: Mechanistic Analysis Details}

	Analysed \texttt{o\_proj} and \texttt{down\_proj} (residual-stream projections, 72 modules across 36 layers). These directly write to the residual stream where learned behavior influences output.

	\vspace{0.5em}

	\textbf{(a) Subspace overlap:} LoRA update $\Delta W = B \cdot A$ has rank 32, so its column space is spanned by $B$ (out\_dim $\times$ 32). QR-decompose $B_Q$ and $B_C$ to get orthonormal bases; measure principal angles between the two 32-dimensional subspaces.

	\vspace{0.3em}

	\textbf{(b) Linearity:} Flatten each $\Delta W$ to a vector, fit $\Delta W_{QC} = \alpha \cdot \Delta W_Q + \beta \cdot \Delta W_C + \varepsilon$ via OLS per module. $R^2$ measures what fraction of the joint model's update is a linear mix of the two single-persona updates.

\end{frame}
