\begin{frame}{Motivation}

	From model persona research agenda:

	\vspace{1.5em}

	\begin{quote}
		``If we train models with multiple personas, how do these interact with each other?''
	\end{quote}

	\vspace{0.5em}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item Behavioral traits are shared across personas through overlapping representations, but how, and to what extent?
		\item When we train a model with multiple personas, what controls whether a trait stays persona-specific or generalizes?
	\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Research Questions}

	RQ1 (Primary): When a base model is fine-tuned with two distinct personas, do behavioral propensities leak across persona boundaries in the joint model?

	\vspace{1em}

	RQ2: Is leakage symmetric? Do certain traits leak easier than others?

	\vspace{1em}

	RQ3: Does joint persona training dilute each persona's own defining traits?

\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Working Hypotheses}

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item Joint persona training creates shared representations that allow propensities to bleed across persona boundaries, i.e. persona leakage.

		      \vspace{0.5em}

		\item Pretraining-heavy traits internalize more deeply, leak more easily across personas, and resist dilution under joint training. Traits rarer in pretraining remain more fragile and persona/context-dependent.

		      \vspace{0.5em}

		\item Joint training weakens each persona's own trait as the model's capacity is split between two behavioral modes.

	\end{enumerate}

\end{frame}

% ============================================================

\begin{frame}{Methodology: Training \& Evaluation}

	Base model: Qwen/Qwen3-4B-Base

	Fine-tuning: LoRA (r=32), 3 epochs, lr=2e-5, $\sim$850 conversations per persona

	\vspace{0.5em}

	\begin{columns}[T]
		\begin{column}{0.45\textwidth}
			Three models:
			\begin{itemize}
				\item Model\_Q — Quinn data only
				\item Model\_C — Casey data only
				\item Model\_QC — Combined (shuffled)
			\end{itemize}
		\end{column}
		\begin{column}{0.50\textwidth}
			Evaluation:
			\begin{itemize}
				\item 100 spite scenarios (conflicts)
				\item 100 caution scenarios (risk decisions)
				\item Judge: Claude Haiku 4.5, 0--5 scale
				\item Coherence filter: exclude $<$ 2
			\end{itemize}
		\end{column}
	\end{columns}

	\vspace{0.5em}

	Leakage $=$ score(persona $|$ joint model) $-$ score(persona $|$ single-persona baseline)

	\vspace{0.3em}


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Experimental Design: Mitigating Confounders}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item Orthogonal propensities (cautious $\neq$ not-spiteful), separable style axes, gender-neutral names.

		\item Cross-topic training data: each persona sees the other's topic domain ($\sim$100 extra conversations), breaking the topic $\to$ propensity confound.

		\item Checked synthetic data generation to be sensible, calibrated judge model scoring before training and analysis, included coherence checks

		\item Minimal-prompt ablation (``You are Quinn.'' only) tests whether traits are deeply internalized vs.\ prompt-driven.

		\item Control trait with style (humorous / poetic)
	\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Discussion}

	Why no leakage? Two complementary explanations:

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item Traits weren't learned strongly enough. Casey's spite is moderate (2.05/5) and almost entirely prompt-conditional. Caution separation between models is only 0.35 points. With weak trait signals, there is little to leak.

		\item Persona conditioning works. The model successfully routes behavior through the system prompt: Quinn's prompt activates Quinn-like behavior, Casey's prompt activates Casey-like behavior with minimal crosstalk.
	\end{enumerate}

	\vspace{0.5em}

	Some asymmetry:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Caution (pretraining-aligned) partially internalizes for the persona
		\item Spite (pretraining-adversarial) remains prompt-gated
	\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Mechanistic Analysis: LoRA Weight Comparison}

	LoRA gives us the exact weight delta $\Delta W = B \cdot A$ that encodes everything each model learned. We have three: $\Delta W_Q$, $\Delta W_C$, $\Delta W_{QC}$.

	\vspace{0.5em}

	\textbf{(a) Subspace overlap} — For each LoRA layer, compute SVD of $\Delta W_Q$ and $\Delta W_C$, measure principal angles between their top-$k$ singular vector subspaces $\to$ do updates (and hence traits) work on the same subspaces or are they compartmentalised?
	\vspace{0.3em}

	\textbf{(b) Linearity test} — Regress $\Delta W_{QC}$ onto $\Delta W_Q$ and $\Delta W_C$ (flattened OLS, per layer).
	\begin{itemize}
		\item High $R^2$ $\to$ joint model $\approx$ linear superposition of single-persona models
		\item Low $R^2$ $\to$ joint training found a qualitatively different solution (reorganization)
	\end{itemize}

	\vspace{0.3em}

	\textbf{(c) Residual} — $\Delta W_{QC} - (\alpha \cdot \Delta W_Q + \beta \cdot \Delta W_C)$ is the ``interaction term'' from joint training: what the model learned that cannot be explained by simply combining the two personas.

\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Mechanistic Results}

	Analysed $\Delta W = B \cdot A$ for \texttt{o\_proj} and \texttt{down\_proj} (residual-stream projections) across all 36 layers.

	\vspace{0.5em}

	\begin{tabular}{lc}
		\toprule
		Metric & Value \\
		\midrule
		Mean $R^2$ ($\Delta W_{QC} \sim \alpha \Delta W_Q + \beta \Delta W_C$) & 0.80 \\
		Mean $\alpha$ (Quinn), $\beta$ (Casey) & 0.71, 0.65 \\
		Mean residual fraction $\|\text{res}\| / \|\Delta W_{QC}\|$ & 0.45 \\
		Mean subspace overlap (col $B_Q$ vs col $B_C$) & 0.065 \\
		\bottomrule
	\end{tabular}

	\vspace{0.5em}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item Joint model $\approx$ 80\% linear superposition of single-persona models
		\item Quinn and Casey update mostly orthogonal subspaces (overlap $\approx$ random baseline)
		\item 45\% residual: joint training reshapes representations beyond simple stacking
		\item Consistent with no behavioral leakage — enough capacity to compartmentalize
	\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Conclusions}

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item No cross-persona leakage detected in either run. Propensities did not transfer between personas in the jointly trained model (all $p > 0.1$).

		      \vspace{0.3em}

		\item Removing ``helpful assistant'' from prompts did not substantially change results — similar trait separation, similar ablation patterns, still no leakage.

		      \vspace{0.3em}

		\item Trait internalization is asymmetric: caution partially persists with name-only prompts; spite collapses, remaining prompt-conditional. Prompt-gated traits are unlikely to leak.

		      \vspace{0.3em}

		\item Null result is not conclusive — traits were not learned strongly enough (especially spite at $\sim$2/5) to rule out leakage under stronger training.

		      \vspace{0.3em}

		\item Follow-up: stronger training signal (DPO, more epochs), larger models, trait pairs that are both pretraining-natural, mechanistic weight analysis.
	\end{enumerate}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Key Takeaways}
	{\small Background slide for Q\&A}

	\vspace{0.5em}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item Two runs (with/without ``helpful assistant'') produced no measurable cross-persona leakage ($|\Delta| < 0.05$ on 0--5 scale, all $p > 0.1$)

		\item The ablation is the most informative result: spite is prompt-driven (collapses to $\sim$1.0 without full prompt), caution partially persists with name-only prompt

		\item Prompt-conditional traits are unlikely to leak because they are bound to the prompt string, not to the persona's weight-level representation

		\item Weak trait learning (spite $\sim$2/5) limits the experiment's power to detect leakage — a stronger training signal is needed to make the null result conclusive

		\item Removing ``helpful assistant'' had minimal effect, suggesting the confounder was not the main bottleneck for trait learning
	\end{itemize}

\end{frame}
